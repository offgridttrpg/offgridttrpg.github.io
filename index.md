# Off the Grid — A TTRPG Resistance Reader

## Preface — The Party Is Over

There was a thread on Google+ in 2013. Someone had posted a half-page RPG: a complete game, rules and all, laid out in plain text inside a post. No PDF. No itch.io page. No cover art. Just the text, and below it a chain of comments from people who had read it, run it, changed it, posted their own variants. The thread had maybe forty participants. It was not covered anywhere. It did not go viral. It existed in that particular way things existed on Google+ in 2013: visible to the people who were already there, invisible to everyone else, circulating on its own internal logic.

I don't know if that specific thread is still archived somewhere. The point is not the thread. The point is what surrounded it: an infrastructure that had not yet decided what to do with tabletop roleplaying games. The algorithm had not found them yet. The conversion funnel did not exist. People posted things because they wanted other people to read them, and other people read them because they wanted to, and the distance between those two desires was very short.

Then Google+ shut down. It happened in stages: an announcement in 2018, the final closure in 2019. The migration that followed was not a simple relocation. People moved to Discord, to Reddit, to Twitter, to their own islands of infrastructure. Some moved to Itch.io, which was already becoming the default marketplace for independent games. Some started Kickstarters. Some started newsletters. The community did not dissolve; it reorganized around new platforms, and those platforms came with different logics.

This reorganization was not neutral. Every platform makes certain things easier and certain things harder. Discord makes real-time conversation easy and archival hard. Reddit makes thread-based discussion easy and minority opinions hard. Itch.io makes sales and discovery easy and obscurity hard; if you publish there, you are implicitly participating in a marketplace, and marketplaces have rules about what succeeds. Kickstarter makes funding easy and slow accumulation hard: your game has a launch window, a campaign, a moment of maximum attention, and then it is over.

What changed was not the games. The games were always there, in every register of ambition and craft and strangeness. What changed was the infrastructure through which they moved, and infrastructure has politics. It rewards certain behaviors and penalizes others. It produces certain kinds of visibility and makes others structurally impossible. The people who succeeded in the new ecosystem were not necessarily the ones making the most interesting games. They were the ones who had learned, or were willing to learn, how to operate within the new logic.

The party that ended was not a golden age. I want to be careful about this. Google+ had its own problems, its own cliques, its own unexamined hierarchies. The OSR blogosphere that preceded it was not some prelapsarian commons. There was always money involved, always status, always the ordinary human machinery of who gets listened to and who doesn't. What was different was the infrastructure's relationship to attention: it had not yet learned to monetize it. The platforms had not yet built the tools to turn community participation into marketing labor. The gap between making something and selling something had not yet been systematically closed.

Something was lost in the translation, and this essay is an attempt to name it precisely.

## I — What a TTRPG Actually Is

Strip it down to the irreducible. Four people around a table. One of them has a notebook and a handful of dice. The others have character sheets, or don't; it doesn't matter much. There is no soundtrack. There is no production value. There is nothing to look at. Someone speaks, and what they say is not quite real and not quite fiction, and everyone in the room accepts this without negotiating it, because the acceptance is the game.

Something emerges from that room that nobody planned. The fighter who was supposed to be a minor plot device becomes, over three sessions, the emotional center of the campaign. The dungeon that was procedurally generated becomes, through the decisions made inside it, a place with a history. The rules that were written to resolve conflict become, in practice, a system for producing the specific texture of surprise that this particular group needs. None of this was in the rulebook. All of it was made possible by the rulebook. The distinction matters enormously.

A tabletop roleplaying game is a relational act. Not a content delivery system, not an entertainment product, not an experience to be consumed: a relational act, something that happens between people and produces something that belongs to no one. The value does not live in the book. The book is a set of conditions. The value lives in what the conditions make possible, which is always something the book did not contain and could not have predicted.

This is what makes the TTRPG structurally resistant to commodification. Not morally resistant: the market has found a hundred ways to extract money from the activity, and will find a hundred more. Structurally resistant: the thing that matters most about the game, the emergence, cannot be packaged. You can sell the conditions. You cannot sell what the conditions produce. The session belongs to the people who were in the room. It is not reproducible, not transferable, not scalable. The moment it ends, it exists only as memory, and memories are not inventory.

The early practitioners of DIY tabletop design understood this, in the untheorized way that people understand things they are too busy doing to analyze. The homebrew table was an intervention in the printed rules, a claim that the printed rules were not the game but raw material for the game. The photocopied zine was the designer saying: I made something from the conditions, and here are conditions of my own, and now you can make something from those. The rules written in the margins of someone else's book were a statement about who the game belonged to, which was: whoever was playing it, right now, with whatever they had on hand.

The game was always already a fork. The canonical version was always a starting point, not a destination. The fiction of the complete, authoritative rulebook, the book that contains the game, was always a fiction, useful for commerce and inconvenient for play. Real play is adaptation. It is the group that doesn't use the morale rules because they don't fit the tone. The GM who changes the magic system halfway through a campaign because something better occurred to her. The players who collectively decided that the dungeon master's secret was obvious and refused to pretend otherwise, and made the game better by doing it.

The market does not sell emergence. It cannot; emergence, by definition, happens in the doing, and the doing is not for sale. So it sells the conditions as if they were the experience. The rulebook becomes the game. The PDF becomes the play. The beautifully laid-out hardcover with the gorgeous art and the carefully balanced subsystems becomes, in the logic of the market, the thing itself, and everything that happens at the table when you open it becomes a downstream effect, a use-case, an activation of the product.

This substitution is not a conspiracy. Nobody sat in a room and decided to deceive the community about where the value lives. It is the natural result of trying to build a market around something that resists marketization. You cannot sell the session. So you sell the book that makes the session possible, and gradually, the book becomes what people talk about, what they buy, what they review, what they display on their shelves. The session, the actual game, the thing that was the point, becomes secondary. It becomes the thing you do, if you get around to it, after you have acquired the conditions.

## II — The Hype Machine

The mechanism is not complicated. A designer announces a game. The announcement generates interest, and that interest is immediately converted into a crowdfunding campaign. The campaign has a funding goal, a deadline, and a sequence of stretch goals that unlock additional content as backing increases. The campaign runs for thirty days. During those thirty days, the designer posts updates: development progress, previews, behind-the-scenes material, and the backers share those updates because they have a stake in the campaign succeeding. The campaign funds. The designer fulfills. The game ships. Within six months, the designer is working on the next campaign.

There is nothing fraudulent in this. People get what they paid for, or close to it, or eventually. The mechanism works, in the narrow sense that money changes hands and products are produced. What it produces, and what it cannot produce, is worth looking at more carefully.

Kickstarter's entry into TTRPG publishing was not a neutral event. Before Kickstarter, the path from game idea to published game was long, uncertain, and largely decoupled from public attention. You wrote the game, you playtested it, you found a way to print or distribute it, and the audience found it over time, or didn't. The timeline was yours. Kickstarter replaced that timeline with a campaign window, and the campaign window changed everything.

Zine Quest began in 2019 as a Kickstarter initiative to support small-format, self-published games, the kind of thing that had always been the engine of indie TTRPG culture, cheap and weird and disposable in the best sense. It was a good idea at the right moment. Within a few cycles it had become a micro-festival, with dozens of simultaneous campaigns competing for the same pool of backer attention during the same thirty-day window. The most polished pitch won, not the most interesting game, but the one with the clearest sell, the most compelling preview images, the most practiced campaign update voice. The format rewarded people who already knew how to run campaigns, which meant it rewarded experience with the platform over quality of the work.

The stretch goal is not a gift. It is a retention tool. It creates a reason to keep promoting a campaign that has already funded, generates additional update posts, and gives backers an investment in momentum beyond their initial decision. The platform's structure does the rest.

Campaign updates as content is the most under-examined part of the cycle. A crowdfunding campaign that runs for thirty days needs to stay visible for thirty days. The designer who is writing campaign updates is not designing the game. The backer who is sharing them is performing marketing labor without compensation, because they have identified with the campaign's success and internalized its promotional logic.

The $20 PDF nobody plays is the endpoint of this cycle. People back campaigns for games they find interesting in concept. They receive the PDF, read some of it, and it joins a folder with dozens of others purchased under similar circumstances. The game is not played. It is owned.

Ownership and play are not the same desire, and the market depends on that confusion. Wanting to own a game is wanting to possess the conditions: to have them available, to signal membership in a community that values this kind of thing. Wanting to play a game is wanting the session, the emergence, the specific thing that happens between four people in a room. These two desires can coexist in the same person without ever converging. You can own a game for three years and never play it. You can play a game for three years using a photocopy of a photocopy with coffee stains on the second page. The market serves one of these people efficiently. It has built an entire infrastructure around the transaction that satisfies them. The other person, the one who actually wants to play, is, from the platform's perspective, an afterthought. They are the use case that happens, or doesn't, after the purchase event is complete.

The hype cycle runs on weeks. Actual play runs on months or years. A campaign that funds in March, fulfills in September, and produces a game that a group plays weekly for the next two years is, from the platform's perspective, a single event: the campaign. Everything after fulfillment is invisible. There is no mechanism in the ecosystem to register, celebrate, or amplify the game that gets played for two years. There is only the mechanism that registers and amplifies the campaign, the launch, the moment of maximum commercial velocity.

The game that circulates slowly has no mechanism to survive in this ecosystem. The game without a launch window, without a campaign, without a thirty-day moment of maximum attention, the game that finds its readers by accident over years through word of mouth and personal recommendation: that game does not exist, from the platform's perspective. It has no metrics. It generates no events. It is invisible in precisely the way that a game well-suited to the actual structure of tabletop play might be.

## III — The Review Is an Ad

The podcast releases an actual play of a new game. Eight episodes, high production value, a cast that is clearly experienced with the system. At the end of episode one, there is a brief mention that the campaign to fund the game is currently running on Kickstarter. In the show notes: a link. No disclosure that the podcast received advance access to the rules, or that one of the cast members has a professional relationship with the designer, or that the campaign team provided promotional assets used in the episode's thumbnail. The episode sounds like a group of friends discovering a new game. It is a sponsored preview with the sponsorship undisclosed.

This is not an isolated case. It is a structural feature of the current ecosystem. The YouTube channel that reviews TTRPG products regularly receives advance copies from publishers. The reviewer mentions this sometimes, in passing, at the end of a video. The Discord server that functions as the de facto community hub for a mid-size indie game is moderated by two people on the publisher's payroll. The newsletter that covers new releases and recommendations is supported by affiliate commissions from the platform it links to most often. None of this is secret, exactly. It is simply not disclosed in the place and form that would allow the reader or viewer to factor it into their evaluation.

Before going further: this is not an argument against getting paid for creative work. The podcaster who spends forty hours producing an actual play deserves compensation. The reviewer who puts genuine craft into a written analysis deserves compensation. The Discord moderator doing community management work deserves compensation. None of that is the problem. The problem is the performance of disinterested opinion where no disinterested opinion exists: what we might call simulated neutrality.

Simulated neutrality is not lying. It is the careful construction of a voice that sounds like it is giving you unbiased information when it is in fact giving you information shaped by a relationship that is not disclosed. The actual play that sounds like genuine discovery when it is a scheduled preview. The review that sounds like an independent assessment when it is built on materials provided by the publisher with an implicit expectation of favorable coverage. The community recommendation that sounds like peer advice when it comes from someone whose relationship with the product being recommended is material and unreported.

The damage simulated neutrality does is not primarily to individual purchasing decisions, though it does that too. The larger damage is epistemic: it degrades the quality of information available to a community over time. When everything is recommended, nothing is evaluated. When the default mode of discourse is enthusiasm underwritten by undisclosed relationships, the community loses the ability to make fine-grained distinctions. Games become indistinguishable from their marketing. The signal-to-noise ratio collapses. And because the community's attention is finite, the games that win the discourse are the ones that can generate the most promotional relationships, not the ones that reward the most sustained engagement.

The structural reason this persists is simple. Visibility in the current ecosystem requires relationships with publishers. Publishers provide advance copies, promotional support, affiliate arrangements, and access to community events. Reviewers and content creators who maintain good relationships with publishers get more content, more traffic, and more income. Reviewers who are critical or who maintain strict disclosure practices get fewer advance copies, less access, and a harder path to financial sustainability. The system selects for simulated neutrality because simulated neutrality is what the system rewards.

There is another kind of writing about games that has no name and no promotional infrastructure, and it has always been the thing that actually works. The session report on a personal blog, written six months after a campaign ended, by someone who ran the game fourteen times. The thread on a small forum where four people who played the game disagree with each other in productive detail about what the third chapter is doing. The message from someone in your extended network who ran the game at a convention and can tell you exactly at what point the resolution mechanic broke down and why. None of these are sponsored. None of them have a financial relationship with anything. They are written by people who played the game and have things to say about it: the oldest and most reliable form of criticism, which the current ecosystem has not eliminated, only buried under the output of people with products to move.

The practical implication is not that you should stop reading reviews. It is that you should learn to read the infrastructure of a recommendation, not just its content. Who is speaking? From what position? What is their relationship to the thing being reviewed: financial, social, promotional? Was the advance copy disclosed? Is the enthusiasm proportional to the evidence being offered, or does it exceed it in a way that suggests a motivation that is not being named? These are not cynical questions. They are the ordinary critical tools that any reader should bring to any text that is trying to get them to do something.

## IV — Open Washing

In January 2023, Wizards of the Coast leaked a draft revision of the Open Game License. The OGL had been, since 2000, the legal instrument through which third-party publishers could produce compatible content for Dungeons & Dragons. The revision proposed to replace it with something substantially less permissive: to retroactively narrow the rights that had been granted, to introduce royalty provisions for successful publishers, to give Wizards effective veto power over what third parties could do with their designs.

The community reaction was fast and large. Publishers pulled their products from WotC-affiliated platforms. A mass cancellation of D&D Beyond subscriptions began. The major indie publishers and community organizations convened rapidly around alternatives: the ORC license, developed by Paizo and other publishers as a genuinely open replacement; Creative Commons licensing for System Reference Documents; public commitments from company after company to release their core rules under CC BY. Within weeks, "open" had become the most politically charged word in tabletop gaming.

What followed was not a philosophical revolution. It was a branding event.

Companies that had never shown particular interest in open licensing rushed to release CC BY versions of their SRDs. The CC label became a trust signal in a spooked market. Publishers understood, correctly, that being visibly open was good for business in the current climate: it differentiated them from WotC, it communicated a set of values the community was actively rewarding. Some of these publishers had genuine commitments to open culture. Many were responding to market incentives with the tools that were available.

This is open washing: the use of open licensing as a marketing instrument, detached from any substantive commitment to the infrastructure of openness. It has a specific profile. The SRD is released under CC BY. The core rulebook remains a polished, proprietary PDF available for purchase. The official character-building tool is a subscription service. The adventure modules, the supplements, the setting materials: all sold through the standard commercial channels, all formatted in ways that are legally open but practically closed. The license says you can share it. The format makes it very difficult to do anything with it.

This distinction, between license openness and technical openness, is the one the community has mostly failed to make. A CC BY PDF is legally open in a precise and meaningful sense: you can copy it, distribute it, modify it, use it commercially, without asking permission, provided you attribute the source. This is genuinely valuable. It is not, however, the same as technical openness. A PDF is a presentation format. It is designed for reading, not for modification. You cannot programmatically extract its structure. You cannot run a script against it to pull out all the tables. You cannot fork it in the way you would fork a software project: take the source, make changes, distribute the changed version as a working artifact. A CC BY PDF is open the way a glass display case is open: you can see everything inside, and you cannot touch any of it.

A plain text file or a Markdown document in a public repository is open in a different way. You can download it, change it, run automated tools against it, generate new formats from it, diff it against previous versions, maintain a fork with your house rules, and do all of this without special software, without proprietary subscriptions, without the original author's involvement. The openness is technical, which means it is operational. You can actually work with it.

The real question is not whether a license is open. It is: open for whom, and open to do what? A CC BY PDF serves the publisher who wants to signal trustworthiness and the player who wants to read the rules without paying. It does not serve the designer who wants to build a tool around the rules. It does not serve the translator who wants to produce a version in another language. It does not serve the community that wants to maintain a living, updatable, forkable version of a game they have been playing for ten years. These use cases require technical openness, and technical openness is not a function of the license; it is a function of the format.

The OGL crisis produced real gains. The ORC license is a substantive improvement. The mass migration toward CC BY SRDs has made a genuine difference to the legal landscape of third-party publishing. None of that should be minimized. What it did not produce is a reckoning with the infrastructure of openness: with the question of what file formats a genuinely open game should be distributed in, what tools should be available to work with it, what it means for a game to be forkable in the technical sense rather than merely the legal one.

Genuine openness is an infrastructure choice. It shows up in whether the game's source is available in a format that can be worked with. In whether the layout is a build artifact generated from structured source, or the source itself. In whether the rules are separated from the fiction, the tables from the prose, in a way that allows the components to be recombined without reassembling the whole document by hand. These are not glamorous design decisions. They do not generate announcement posts or campaign updates. They are the difference between a game that can be forked and a game that merely says it can be.

## V — The Creator Ecosystem Trap

At some point in the last decade, the advice consolidated. If you are a serious indie TTRPG designer, you maintain a Substack or newsletter. You have a Patreon with at least one tier. You are consistently active on at least two social media platforms. You have a recognizable visual brand: a logo, a consistent color palette, a cover aesthetic that makes your work identifiable at a glance. You show up regularly enough that your audience does not forget you exist between releases.

This advice did not arrive from nowhere. It emerged from the legitimate problem of sustainable creative work. The economics of indie game publishing are brutal, and the tools of the creator economy, recurring subscriptions and direct audience relationships and content marketing, do offer real paths to financial sustainability for people who use them well. The advice was given by people who had made it work, to people who wanted to make it work, and in that original context it was often sound.

What happened next is what happens to useful advice in a community with strong social norms and a tendency toward imitation: it calcified. It went from "here is one way to sustain creative work economically" to "here is what serious designers do." It stopped being a strategy and became a checklist. And the checklist is now applied without regard to goals, context, or what the designer actually wants from their work.

The creator ecosystem framework imports the logic of the attention economy wholesale into a space where that logic is actively destructive. The attention economy rewards visibility over quality, because visibility can be measured and quality is contested. It rewards consistency over experimentation, because a regular publishing schedule trains an algorithm and an audience alike, while an irregular one that produces genuinely surprising work is hard to optimize. It rewards audience-building over game-building, because the audience is a resource that compounds over time while the game is a one-time output. None of these are neutral aesthetic preferences. They are the structural incentives of platforms built to monetize attention, and they produce predictable results when applied to creative work.

The damage is specific and it compounds. Newsletter time is not time stolen from nothing; it is time stolen from playtesting, from revision, from the slow work of figuring out whether the game actually does what it is supposed to do. The decision to make a one-page game dies in the moment you realize you cannot build a content calendar around it. The strange and valuable creative choice gives way to the safe one, the one that comes with a built-in explanation of why it matters, the one that will make a coherent announcement post. The creator ecosystem does not prohibit experimentation. It just makes experimentation legible as risk, and then it makes risk feel like something a serious designer does not take.

The political claim needs to be stated directly: the right to make games without building a brand is not a failure mode. It is a legitimate position. A designer who makes games they find interesting, shares them in whatever way is convenient, and does not maintain a consistent social media presence is not doing it wrong. They are making a choice about how to spend their creative energy that is different from the choice the creator ecosystem framework recommends, and that difference is not a deficiency.

Commercial invisibility is not the same as irrelevance. A game that circulates among fifty people who play it seriously and return to it over years has produced something real and durable. A game that has ten thousand Itch.io page views, a funded Kickstarter, and a Patreon of three hundred subscribers may or may not have produced anything that gets played at all. The metrics count one of these things and not the other, and because the metrics are what the ecosystem amplifies, the ecosystem makes the second look like success and the first look like failure.

The counter-model exists, even if it has no name and no promotional infrastructure. Games circulate by email, passed between people who have played together. They are posted on personal websites with no comment section, no follower count, no analytics dashboard. They are distributed at conventions as physical artifacts, without a QR code pointing to a Patreon. They are shared in Discord servers too small to have a promoted post, or in forums so old the interface looks like a design mistake. These games find their people. They do not find many people, and they do not find them quickly, and by every metric the ecosystem cares about they do not exist.

If you make a game and nobody follows you for it, did the game fail? The creator ecosystem has a clear answer, and the answer is built into its infrastructure: yes, because success is measured in reach, and reach is what the platform produces. But this answer assumes that the platform's measure is the right measure: that the value of a game is a function of how many people it touched in a trackable way. That assumption is doing an enormous amount of work, mostly invisible. A game played by eight people over two years, shaping how they think about conflict and consequence and each other, has produced something. The metric for that something does not exist. The absence of the metric is not the absence of the thing.

## VI — Forkable by Design

Consider two games, both "available."

The first is a 120-page full-color PDF, $25 on DriveThruRPG. It has a striking cover, professional interior layout, custom fonts, a table of contents, bookmarks. The art is commissioned, atmospheric, consistent with the game's tone. The design is good. Reading it is a pleasure. The license page says it is released under CC BY 4.0.

The second is a plain-text document in a public GitHub repository. No cover. No layout beyond headings and lists. The font is whatever your text editor uses. The license is the same: CC BY 4.0. The game is shorter, thirty pages maybe, and the writing is direct and undecorated.

Both are legally open. What you can actually *do* with them is not the same at all.

With the PDF: you can read it. You can share a link to it. You can print it. If you want to modify it, correcting an error, rewriting a section, translating it into another language, you must either start from scratch, recreate the layout in software you may not own, or work from an OCR extraction that will mangle tables and break formatting. If you want to build a tool around it, a character generator or reference app or rules wiki, you must parse a format designed for visual presentation, not data extraction. The CC label says you can do these things. The format makes most of them extremely difficult.

With the Markdown file, the operations that were theoretical with the PDF become actual. You can edit the text directly, without reproducing the layout, without converting anything. You can fork the repository, make changes, and redistribute a version that works, not a description of your modifications, but a complete, functional game that embodies them. You can write a script that pulls every table out and formats them as reference cards. You can generate a PDF, or an ePub, or a web page, from the same source, at will. You can maintain your house-rules variant alongside the original, see exactly where they diverge, track changes over time. None of this requires special software. None of it requires asking permission. The document is not a presentation of the game; it is material you can work with.

This is what forkable design means. Not a legal permission but an operational reality: someone else can take the game's components, modify them, redistribute a changed version, and build tools around it, without obstacles that make the permission theoretical. The fork is possible in practice, not just in principle.

Forkability is the technical implementation of what "free culture" was always supposed to mean. The difference between saying "you can share this" and "here is a thing you can actually work with." The copyleft tradition in software made this distinction clearly from the start: a license is not freedom, it is a condition for freedom. The actual freedom lives in the source code, in the availability of something you can build from, not just receive. TTRPG culture arrived at open licensing without arriving at this understanding, because the community took its model from publishing rather than from software, and publishing is built around artifacts rather than source.

What forkable design looks like in practice is not mysterious. Plain text or Markdown for the rules, because these formats are editable in anything and processable by anything. Semantic structure, headings that mean something, lists that are actually lists rather than visual conventions, tables that are actually tables rather than images of tables, because semantic structure is what allows automated tools to traverse the document. Version control history as documentation: if you can read the commits, you can read the history of design decisions, and that history is more honest than any designer note. Separation of rules from fiction from procedure, because these components have different downstream uses and conflating them makes both harder to work with.

The objection about aesthetics mistakes the source for the artifact. Software is not shipped as source code; it is compiled, built, packaged. The source and the release are different things produced from the same material. A beautifully laid-out PDF can be generated from a plain-text Markdown source, with the layout applied as a build step. Several designers already work this way.

The layout is a build artifact, not the game itself.

The choice of file format is a political choice. A locked PDF makes a claim about who the game belongs to: the publisher, the designer, the aesthetic vision that produced it. It can be read. It cannot be changed. A plain-text file in a public repository makes a different claim: the game belongs to whoever can work with it, which is anyone with a text editor. In a community that talks constantly about openness, creativity, and the free exchange of ideas, the infrastructure of openness deserves at least as much attention as the rhetoric of it.

## VII — Off the Grid

"Off the grid" does not mean without technology. It means without the specific kind of technology built to produce metrics: to count, to record, to report, to convert activity into data that someone else can use. Writing in Markdown and publishing on a static site is off the grid. The site exists; people can find it; nothing about visiting it is tracked in a way that feeds an advertising system or a content recommendation engine. Publishing the same text on Substack is not off the grid: every open is counted, every click recorded, every pattern of engagement fed back into a system designed to help you produce more content that generates more engagement. The technology is similar. The infrastructure is entirely different.

The right to invisibility is not a consolation prize for people who couldn't make it on the platforms. It is a position with its own logic and its own value. To play without posting about it. To design without an analytics dashboard telling you which ideas performed well. To publish without a launch strategy, without a newsletter blast, without a week of scheduled social posts. To make something and give it to whoever finds it, and not know who that is, and not need to know. This is not asceticism. It is a refusal to let the infrastructure define the value of the activity.

Off-grid TTRPG practice is not hypothetical. It exists, in forms that the mainstream discourse cannot easily see because they generate no events. The solo player who journals a campaign in plain text files, never posted, never shared, as a record for themselves alone. The GM who runs a homebrew system they built over three years, modified from seven different games, that lives in a folder on their computer and nowhere else. The designer who shared a game as an email attachment to twelve people they knew, and one of those people shared it with twelve more, and the game found a hundred players without ever appearing on a single platform. The group that documents their campaign in a private wiki, for their own reference, with no comment section and no audience.

These practices exist. They are less connected to the broader community; the off-grid designer misses the feedback that comes from iteration with strangers, the off-grid player steps outside a discourse that, for all its problems, does transmit genuine knowledge about how games work. These are real costs. The point is not to pretend otherwise. The point is that the current discourse presents the off-grid position not as a trade-off with costs and benefits, but as an absence, a failure to participate, a falling-short. That framing serves the platform, not the designer. The off-grid position is, plainly, a choice, one the ecosystem has made nearly invisible not through prohibition, but through the more efficient mechanism of making its absence seem like failure. The platform ecosystem benefits from the assumption that visibility equals value, because visibility is what the platform produces and sells. Every designer who internalizes this assumption becomes a volunteer in the platform's promotional infrastructure, generating content, building audiences, creating the activity that makes the platform valuable to advertisers and investors. The assumption should be named. It is not a description of reality. It is a claim with interested parties.

What the off-grid position recovers is a relationship to the activity on its own terms. Play that is not instrumentalized toward content production. Design that is not shaped by what will communicate well in a campaign update. Publishing that is not timed to a launch window or optimized for discoverability. These are not trivial goods. The pressure to produce content about your practice, to document it and share it in ways that generate engagement, is also a pressure to experience it differently, to be slightly outside it even as you are in it, arranging it for the feed.

The solo journaler who writes in a plain text file, with no reader in mind, is not impoverished relative to the person who posts their session reports on a blog with a thousand subscribers. They are engaged with the game in a different way, and the difference is not simply one of scale. The relation to the activity is different when the activity is not being performed for an audience. This is not a new observation. It applies to any practice that can be spectated. What is new is the degree to which the infrastructure now makes spectatorship feel mandatory.

The game happens between people, in a room, in a moment. It does not require documentation to have occurred. It does not require an audience to have mattered. The session that left no record anywhere, no post, no podcast, no actual play, no review, no campaign update, was not less real than the one that did. The campaign that ran for three years and was witnessed only by the people who played it was not diminished by its invisibility. It happened. That is enough.

## Coda — A Message in a Bottle

The TTRPG space is vast, noisy, and largely indifferent to texts like this one. There are podcasts with listener counts that dwarf anything a long-form critical essay will ever reach. There are Discord servers larger than small cities. There are Kickstarters that fund in minutes and newsletters that go out to sixty thousand subscribers. In that context, this text is not an intervention in the discourse. It is a note slipped under a door.

That is the point.

This is not written for the space. It is written for the individual who finds it, in the right moment, already half-convinced. The person who backed a campaign last month and has not opened the PDF. The designer who spent four hours on a newsletter post and knows, in a way they haven't said aloud, that those were not the four most useful hours they could have spent. The player who runs a homebrew game that nobody outside the group has ever heard of and feels, vaguely and without quite knowing why, that this is something to be apologized for.

This text was not announced. It has no campaign page, no newsletter, no Itch.io listing. It is a plain file, on a minimal page, distributed by whatever slow and unmeasured means it reaches you. The form closes the argument the content makes.

If it found you, it found you. That is enough.
